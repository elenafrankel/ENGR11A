Final Project

Improving Door Accessibility on Campus

Learn More

Interviewee Profiles

Interviewee 1: Brandeis student from New Jersey who has injured their wrist in the past. Their injury came from past rock climbing and volleyball incidents that have resulted in lasting pain and mobility problems. Due to their wrist injury and residual effects (such as pain), they struggle to open door knobs with their injured hand. The solutions they had found in their daily life have been using their other (non-dominant) hand, and preferring doors with levers or push operation.

Interviewee 2: Brandeis student from Massachusetts with a history of shoulder injuries from various athletic pursuits. (Dodgeball, volleyball, quadball) Has had 2 surgeries to prevent future dislocations of the shoulder. They don’t find door knobs too inconvenient, but agreed that push doors and handles were definitely preferable options.

Interviewee 3: Brandeis rugby player who injured their wrist over time while playing. They have not been to a doctor, but notice their injury getting worse when falling/tackling and catching. They have door knobs in their dorm, and will often use their non-dominant hand (not injured) to open doors to avoid pain.

Thematic Analysis and Insight Statements

Door knobs are included in both residential and academic building design, but are not universal between buildings.

People with wrist injuries or limited arm movement prefer pushing motions over twisting/pulling.

People with access to 3D printers may or may not have access to the correct hardware to supplement our various design ideas.

Many people appreciate door designs that avoid germ transmission/don’t require the use of hands to open.

It is important to include access to a keyhole for door knobs with locking mechanisms.

Interviewees expressed interest in a semi-permanently installed solution over a portable system.

It is important for the doorknob to work on either side of a door, as well as on differently shaped door knobs.

"How Might We" Statements and Brainstorming

How might we adapt a design for multiple types of doorknobs?

How might we combine the idea of a foot operated door with an adapted door knob?

How might we weigh accessibility of materials with durability and performance?

How might we work with DCL to ensure our solution complies with their rules and safety regulations of the state?

Narrowing it down and final idea selection

After much brainstorming, we decided to move forward with the second print, a clamp design. This design goes around a door knob and clamps down, using a tightening mechanism of a nut and screw. We are hoping to be able to modify the design to be more resilient by using a different material, and improve the design so it cannot be accidentally pulled off of the door knob. This should create an easily accessible file that any Brandeis student can print at the Makerlab on campus, and modify their dorm to be more accessible.
